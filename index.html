When parking lots become party destinations...

Welcome to our Targeted Editing series where today you can cheer on your favorite sports team, or make OpenStreetMap data supreme! (Both take about the same amount of time.)

Here in San Francisco, Super Bowl City is attracting football fans from far and wide. Firing up a barbecue grill in a stadium parking lot as part of a tailgate party is an American pastime. Whether you are here in the Bay Area for the game, or you have plans to attend games at other stadiums this year, you are going to appreciate being able to find the parking areas on a map!

Read on for some basic uses for parking areas and parking aisles, and where you can check to see if you can use your local knowlege to add some of these features where they are needed.

  1. How are parking areas and parking aisles used?

    1. Help with search. Finding parking is often a big part of trip planning.

    2. Help with routing. While your final destination may be a point of interest, you are likely driving to that location's associated parking area. When parking areas also have parking aisles connected to the road network, they can be used to route drivers in and out of parking areas.

    3. Help with map display. Parking lots are valuable features that drivers rely on, and parking aisles look beautiful on a map.

  3. How you can help improve stadium parking:

    1. Hover over one of the bright blue highlighted stadiums to bring up an info bubble with links to editing tools that can be used to add features. These highlighted stadiums are missing parking aisles.


      2. ( Open this map in a new tab ➹ )

        See the wiki pages for amenity=parking, service=parking_aisle, and oneway.

        oneway=yes can be added to parking aisles when the flow of traffic is restricted to one direction. The direction will be the direction in which the parking aisle is digitized.

        For existing parking aisles, if the direction is incorrect, use the “reverse way” tool in iD to reverse the direction of the line to match the flow of oneway traffic. This may also require adding additional nodes to the way to ensure that tags are applied to the appropriate segments.

        Last but not least, make sure parking aisles are connected to the road network in the appropriate locations.

        Including your sources with the source key is incredibly helpful. This includes the imagery you used to trace features, and any authoritative websites as long as they allow their use for this purpose.

        (If you find stadium that appears to have a parking area and parking aisles, but it's still highlighted, you may have found a duplicate, or some other interesting tagging variation. Improving those is a topic for another post...)

    2. Not familiar with the San Francisco Bay Area? Search for your home town or one near it and pan over to an area you know.
